Internal research memo — MLB game-outcome modeling

How GamePredictor works, and what six seasons of walk-forward testing found

GamePredictor forecasts a game's winner and each team's expected runs directly from pitch-by-pitch Statcast history — not box-score averages. This report walks through every stage as it stands today: what data feeds the model, how much of a player's past the model actually sees at prediction time, how the model is trained and validated, and what the results say so far, including a newly added comparison against the real Vegas closing line.

01

Data pipeline

Everything starts from raw, pitch-by-pitch Statcast data — the same tracking data Baseball Savant publishes for every MLB pitch — pulled directly from the source rather than derived from published box scores.

Ingestion

fetch_statcast.py pulls one season at a time in monthly chunks (March through November, capped at the current date for an in-progress season) via pybaseball, and writes one untouched Parquet file per season. Raw files exist back to 2010, but pitch-tracking metadata — spin rate specifically — wasn’t recorded before 2015, so every 2010–2014 row fails validation later in the pipeline and is never used. In practice, the model’s entire world is 2015 onward.

Cleaning & labeling

Each raw pitch is reduced to the columns the model actually needs and given a single outcome label — one of 13 categories (strikeout, walk, hit-by-pitch, single, double, triple, home run, an in-play out, a ball, a called strike, a swinging strike, a foul, or unknown) derived from Statcast’s own events/description fields. Rows missing a critical field (a release speed, a spin rate, an outcome) are flagged, not silently dropped, so exactly how much of a season is unusable stays visible rather than hidden.

What counts as a game

Only regular-season and postseason games count (game_type R, F/D/L/W). Spring training games get swept in by the March–April pull window and are explicitly filtered back out — spring rosters and performance aren’t representative of the real season, and letting them in would quietly corrupt both training and the ERA/wRC+ baseline’s running averages.

Derived tables

From the cleaned pitches, one row per game (game_pk) is built with the final score, the winner, the month, both starting pitchers, and each starter’s days of rest. The starting pitcher and starting lineup for each side aren’t pulled from a schedule feed — they’re read back out of the game’s own pitch data (whoever threw the first pitch; the first 9 distinct batters to reach the plate). That’s legitimate pre-game knowledge to use, since real lineups are announced well before first pitch — it’s just sourced after the fact rather than from a live feed.

02

Model architecture

The system is built in two phases: a PlayerEncoder pretrained once on raw pitch sequences, then reused inside GamePredictor, which combines both teams’ personnel into a single game forecast.

Phase A — PlayerEncoder

A 4-layer, 4-head Transformer encoder (hidden size 128, feedforward 512) that reads one player’s own chronological pitch history — as either the pitcher who threw each pitch or the batter who saw it — and pools it into a single 128-dimensional embedding via a prepended CLS token, the same recipe BERT uses to pool a sentence. Each pitch feeds in as four continuous features (release speed, spin rate, plate-x, plate-z, all z-scored) plus embeddings for pitch type, outcome, the batter/pitcher handedness matchup, and position in the sequence. A player with no qualifying history yet never runs through attention at all — they get one learned “no-history” embedding instead.

It’s pretrained on a genuinely hard task: predict a pitch’s outcome from that same pitcher’s pitches strictly before it — a 13-way classification most of which is actually decided by the batter and the situation, not the pitcher’s own history alone. After 9 epochs (train 2015–2022, validate 2023) it reaches ~36% training / ~35.7% validation accuracy — comfortably above chance, and, as section 04 shows, not a throwaway step.

Pooling a roster

A bullpen or a starting lineup is a variable-size, unordered set of players, not a sequence, so it’s pooled differently: a single learned query attends over each member’s PlayerEncoder embedding (4-head attention), letting the model weight some players more than others — a closer differently from a mop-up reliever, say — rather than just averaging them. An empty set (no bullpen activity yet, an incomplete lineup) gets its own learned “empty set” embedding rather than an all-zero vector.

Phase B — GamePredictor

Six pooled embeddings — home/away starter, home/away bullpen, home/away lineup — are concatenated with a 7-dimensional pre-game context vector (775 dimensions total) and pushed through a 2-layer MLP trunk (hidden size 256, ReLU, dropout 0.1) into three heads: a win-probability logit, and a negative-binomial runs distribution (mean + dispersion) for each team — negative-binomial rather than Poisson because a single game’s run total is noisier than a Poisson assumption allows. The context vector is month (encoded as sin/cos, since a season is cyclical), each starter’s rest days (z-scored, with a missing-flag for a pitcher’s first career start), and a post_humidor flag (2022 onward, when MLB mandated a humidor leaguewide — previously only Coors Field had one).

Training loss is the sum of three terms: binary cross-entropy on the win outcome, plus the negative log-likelihood of each team’s actual run total under its predicted negative-binomial distribution.

03

Training method & how much history each prediction sees

Every input is filtered to strictly before the game being predicted — nothing about how a game actually played out is visible to its own prediction — but different parts of the model look back different distances.

Lookback window, by feature
FeatureWindowIn practice
Starter / batter pitch history 200 pitches A count, not a date range — roughly 6–10 starts back for an everyday starter, a few weeks for an everyday batter. Older history simply isn’t seen; there’s no long-run “career profile” feature.
Bullpen availability 14 days Trailing calendar window of who has actually pitched for that team, excluding that game’s own starter — a proxy for “who’s active right now,” since no historical day-by-day roster feed exists.
Starting lineup that game First 9 distinct batters to actually reach the plate for each side. Pinch-hitters/subs are excluded on purpose — including them would leak in-game information (why was a sub used?) into a pre-game feature.
Model training window (fixed split) 8 seasons 2015–2022 train, 2023 validate, 2024–2025 held out entirely.
Model training window (walk-forward) 5 seasons A fresh model is retrained from scratch on a rolling 5-year train / 1-year validation window for each of the 6 folds — see section 05.

Two-stage fine-tuning

GamePredictor doesn’t train its PlayerEncoder from scratch: it loads the Phase A pretrained checkpoint and fine-tunes in two stages — the encoder stays frozen for the first 5 epochs while the trunk, heads, and set poolers warm up (predictor learning rate 1e-3), then the encoder unfreezes for the remainder at a much lower rate (1e-5), so the much smaller game-outcome dataset (thousands of games vs. millions of pitches) doesn’t overwrite what pretraining learned. Training runs up to 25 epochs with early stopping (patience 4) on validation loss.

Pretraining ablation

Strip out the pretrained encoder and train the exact same architecture from a random initialization, and accuracy drops from 54.35% to 52.29% — worse than the two-feature logistic-regression baseline it’s meant to beat. On this dataset, pretraining isn’t a nice-to-have: it’s the difference between a competitive model and one that can’t clear the simplest bar.

Is any of this actually significant?

A paired bootstrap (1,000 resamples of the same 4,949 games) says: not quite, for GamePredictor vs. the LR baseline alone — the accuracy gap’s 95% interval is −1.3 to +1.5 percentage points, straddling zero. What is significant: the 50/50 average ensemble’s Brier score is reliably better than the LR baseline alone (95% CI entirely below zero), and the stacked-logistic-regression ensemble edges out the plain average on accuracy in 93.1% of resamples — suggestive, just short of the 95% bar.

Consistent, not a fluke

The Vegas closing line beats GamePredictor on calibration (Brier score) in 5 of 6 folds and on raw accuracy in 4 of 6 — with the same direction, just short of significance, in the remaining folds. This holds across test seasons 2021 through 2026, each scored by an independently retrained model, so it isn’t an artifact of any one season.

06

The Vegas closing-line baseline

Added this round: a fourth baseline built from real market prices, so the model can be measured against something with actual money behind it, not just other statistical proxies.

Source & matching

A third-party historical odds export (MLB_Basic.csv, 42,277 rows, 2009–2026: date, teams, final score, and open/close moneyline + total odds) has no shared game ID with the Statcast pipeline, so every row is matched to the games table by date + home team + away team after normalizing team codes through a small crosswalk — the source file uses several retired/renamed abbreviations the Statcast pipeline doesn’t (ARI/AZ, OAK/ATH, FLO/MIA, SLC/STL, CHW/CWS, WAS/WSH).

A match is only accepted if it’s unambiguous (no doubleheader collision on either side) and verified — the joined row’s final score has to match exactly. Anything ambiguous or unverified is routed to a review file instead of guessed at: 26,011 of 42,277 source rows were accepted; the rest were mostly seasons the games table doesn’t yet cover (2009–2014, 14,828 rows), plus 840 doubleheader collisions, 536 genuine non-matches (mostly spring-training games the pipeline deliberately excludes), 50 real score mismatches, and 12 MLB All-Star Game rows.

Removing the vig

A moneyline’s raw implied probability (100÷(odds+100) for an underdog, −odds÷(−odds+100) for a favorite) sums to more than 1.0 across both sides — that overage is the sportsbook’s built-in edge, the vig. Both sides are normalized by that sum so they land on a true probability pair summing to exactly 1.0 before comparing against the model.

07

What we’ve learned

Four findings, consistent across both the fixed split and all 6 walk-forward folds:

1
Pretraining is load-bearing

Without the pretrained PlayerEncoder, GamePredictor loses to the simple ERA/wRC+ logistic regression outright (52.3% vs. 54.2%). The deep architecture only becomes competitive once the encoder has already learned something about pitch-to-pitch patterns from millions of pitches, independent of the much smaller game-outcome signal.

2
GamePredictor ≈ the simple baseline

With pretraining, GamePredictor is statistically indistinguishable from the two-feature ERA/wRC+ baseline — not once, but in every one of the 6 walk-forward folds (0/6 significant either direction on accuracy). All the added machinery — pitch-level transformers, attention-pooled rosters — hasn’t yet bought a measurable edge over two expanding averages plus home/away.

3
Ensembling helps a little

Blending GamePredictor with the LR baseline — especially via a small learned (stacked) combination rather than a plain average — is the best single-point result seen so far (55.0% accuracy on the fixed split) and reliably improves calibration (Brier score) over either model alone.

4
The market is still ahead

The actual Vegas closing line beats both GamePredictor and the LR baseline by a wide, consistent margin — 3–6 points of accuracy, significant on Brier score in 5 of 6 folds. That gap is the clearest signal in this whole report: there’s real predictive information in this problem that neither of the two models built here is capturing yet.
